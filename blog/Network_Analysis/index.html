Everyone knows about social networks like twitter, facebook, and linkedin, but there are many other types of networks that people don't often think about. Airports, financial institutions and governments are just a few examples of networks whose connections arise based off of different sets of rules than that of a social network. In the most general sense, a network is a system of interconnected objects. These objects can be anything, but for it to be a network, these objects need to interact with each other in some fashion. Networks arise in many disciplines and understanding them is of utmost importance. With a branch of mathematics known as graph theory, we can get a better understanding of these networks.

Networks have become a standard tool for analyzing the relationships/interactions between the components of a complex system. When it comes to understanding and modeling certain systems, like protein-protein interactions or gene regulatory networks, it becomes apparent that these systems portray non-linear behavior and are simply too complex to model with a simple mathematical equation.

By utilizing a branch of mathematics known as graph theory, we can produce simple visual representations of these kinds of systems by treating the components of the system as vertices and the relationships between them as edges.
